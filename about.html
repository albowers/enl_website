About the EEG Neurofeedback Lab (ENL)

The EEG Neurofeedback Laboratory at the University of Arkansas is dedicated to advancing our understanding of stuttering through electrophysiological research. Dr. Andrew Bowers, Ph.D. CCC-SLP leads these efforts.

EEG neurofeedback is simply a method for training brain activity by providing feedback in real time, with the goal of reinforcing behavioral cognitive, or emotional goals. It can be combined with behavioral and cognitive therapies and can used synergistically with brain stimulation approaches. The main advantages of EEG for neurofeedback are that is relatively inexpensive, portable, and completely noninvasive. Despite these advantages, developing effective brain-based treatments for stuttering requires overcoming both conceptual and technical barriers. Conceptually, we're developing new frameworks to better understand the neurological basis of stuttering. Technically, we're using innovative approaches to EEG data analysis that address the unique challenges of studying stuttering and other neurodiverse conditions affecting communication. We employ an interdisciplinary approach that combines methods from speech-language pathology, cognitive neuroscience, and biomedical engineering to address these complex challenges.

Because only a handful of labs are working on similar problems, we believe it's critical to leverage collaborative resources, basic research from larger labs, and the broader stuttering community to build on the work of others toward our goals. You can explore collaborating EEG labs below and find links to other influential research groups and community resources in the Links section. The Blog page highlights news, students, and publications of interest from other labs.

Research Focus

Current ENL research is centered around several key areas:

  • Beta Oscillatory Dynamics: Investigating the role of beta oscillations and how alterations in these rhythms may contribute to stuttering. Current investigations are focused on the development of relatively new burst like measures of beta activity in stuttering
  • Event-related phase amplitude coupling: Exploring links between brain rhythms and how they relate to speech motor control and stuttering.
  • Phonological working memory in stuttering: Examining how sensorimotor and auditory oscillations are integrated in phonological working memory and how this process may differ in stuttering.
  • Tools Development: Generating code and data useful for the development of novel analysis and brain-computer interface approaches
  • Measure development: Using mixed methods approaches to generate measures of interoceptive awareness in stuttering

Collaborating EEG labs:

Work in the ENL is supported by collaborating with other EEG labs. Many of these labs are also working on identifying EEG biomarkers in stuttering. Collaborations are important for bringing together work in the labs with clinical services, conceptual frameworks, and a better understanding of how stuttering is related to aspects of language.

Lab Facilities

EEG Recording Suite

Our primary data collection space features a sound-attenuated booth with a 64-channel Electrical Geodesics EEG system. The suite is equipped for simultaneous recording of EEG, EMG, and autonomic measures during speech tasks.

University of Arkansas Speech and Hearing Clinic

The ENL lab is located in the University of Arkansas Speech and Hearing clinic with state of the art facilities for assessment and interviation of communicaiton disorders
