Neurofeedback for Stuttering: Current Progress and Future Directions

Andrew Bowers May 19, 2025 5 Comments Research Updates

Our lab has been making significant strides in the development and testing of neurofeedback protocols for developmental stuttering. In this post, I'd like to share some of our recent progress and discuss where we see this research heading in the next few years.

The premise of neurofeedback for stuttering is to give individuals real-time feedback about their brain activity, specifically sensorimotor beta oscillations (15-30 Hz) that have been implicated in speech motor control. By visualizing these oscillations and providing rewards for specific patterns, participants can learn to voluntarily modulate their brain activity in ways that may support more fluent speech production.

Current Progress

Over the past year, we've completed a pilot study with 12 adults who stutter, implementing a 12-session neurofeedback protocol targeting sensorimotor beta oscillations. The preliminary results are promising:

  • Participants showed significant improvement in stuttering severity as measured by the Stuttering Severity Instrument-4 (SSI-4).
  • We observed meaningful changes in resting-state beta power following the intervention.
  • Self-reported speech confidence and reduced anxiety were reported by most participants.
  • Benefits were maintained at a 3-month follow-up for 75% of participants.

Figure 1: Changes in stuttering severity following neurofeedback intervention. Lower scores indicate reduced stuttering severity.

One of the most interesting findings has been the relationship between changes in beta burst dynamics and improvements in fluency. Participants who showed the greatest reduction in stuttering severity also demonstrated more normalized timing of beta bursts during speech preparation.

Technical Implementation

Our current neurofeedback protocol uses a 16-channel EEG system with electrodes positioned primarily over sensorimotor cortical regions. We've developed a custom software interface that provides real-time feedback through both visual and auditory channels:

  • Visual Feedback: A dynamic display shows beta power as a moving line graph, with a target range highlighted. When the participant's beta activity falls within the optimal range, the display changes color.
  • Auditory Feedback: Pleasant tones play when the participant maintains beta activity within the target range for sustained periods.

Sessions are structured to include periods of rest, speech preparation, and speech production, with feedback parameters adjusted based on individual baseline measurements. The protocol gradually increases in difficulty over the 12 sessions as participants gain more control over their neural activity.

Challenges and Solutions

Developing effective neurofeedback interventions comes with several challenges:

Signal Quality

EEG is notoriously susceptible to movement artifacts, which is particularly problematic when targeting speech production. We've addressed this through:

  • Improved electrode design and attachment methods
  • Advanced real-time artifact detection algorithms
  • Strategic protocol design that separates feedback periods from actual speech production

Individual Variability

We've observed substantial variability in both baseline neural activity and response to neurofeedback training. Our solution has been to implement individualized protocols with:

  • Personalized frequency bands based on each participant's individual beta peak
  • Adaptive threshold adjustments that respond to performance
  • Tailored reinforcement schedules based on learning rate

Transfer to Everyday Situations

A common critique of neurofeedback is whether improvements transfer to real-world situations. We're addressing this by:

  • Gradually incorporating realistic speech tasks into later training sessions
  • Providing "homework" practices to use between sessions
  • Including a transfer phase where feedback is gradually withdrawn

Future Directions

Building on our preliminary success, we're expanding our research in several key directions:

Larger Clinical Trial

We've recently secured funding for a larger randomized controlled trial with 40 adults who stutter. This study will include a sham neurofeedback control group to address placebo effects and will implement a more comprehensive assessment battery.

Neurofeedback for Children

Given the potential for neuroplasticity in younger populations, we're developing a modified protocol suitable for children who stutter, with age-appropriate feedback displays and gamified elements to maintain engagement.

Home-Based Training

To improve accessibility, we're exploring the feasibility of home-based neurofeedback using consumer-grade EEG devices. While these systems have limitations in signal quality, they may serve as a supplement to in-lab sessions or as a maintenance option after completing the primary intervention.

Multimodal Approaches

We're also investigating combined interventions that integrate neurofeedback with traditional speech therapy approaches. This multimodal strategy may provide synergistic benefits by addressing both the neural underpinnings and behavioral aspects of stuttering.

Conclusion

The potential of neurofeedback for stuttering intervention is promising but still evolving. Our work aims to establish a solid evidence base through rigorous research while simultaneously refining protocols to maximize efficacy and accessibility.

We're excited about the possibilities this approach offers for individuals who stutter and are committed to continuing this line of research. If you're interested in learning more or potentially participating in our upcoming studies, please contact our lab.

I'll be sharing updates on our research progress periodically on this blog, so stay tuned for more developments!

About the Author: Dr. Andrew Bowers

Andrew Bowers, Ph.D., is an Associate Professor at the University of Arkansas and Director of the EEG Neurofeedback Laboratory. His research focuses on sensorimotor integration in speech perception and production, with a particular emphasis on developmental stuttering. Dr. Bowers has published extensively on EEG methodologies, beta oscillations, and neurofeedback applications.
