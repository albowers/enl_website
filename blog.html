Savanah Green completes undergraduate honors thesis

Andrew Bowers May 19, 2025 5 Comments Student Highlights

We want to congratulate Savanah on the completion of her honors thesis. She collected and helped to analyze data from over 70 respondents to a survey asking questions about what stuttering feels like, how individuals define it, and what fluency feels like. The responses from the qualtive response from the survey that Savanah helped to analze also led to other graduate student projects, incliding a master's thesis project developing a questionairre from the responses. We look forward to everything Savanah will do as she works toward her masters's degree in speech-language pathology at the University of Central Arkansas!

Leslie Williams is working on a questionnaire tool for her master's thesis about interoceptive awareness of stuttering

Andrew Bowers April 28, 2025 8 Comments Student Highlights

Leslie is a graduate student working on her master's in speech-language pathology at the University of Arkansas. Leslie's project focuses on developing a questionnaire tool to assess interoceptive awareness in individuals who stutter. This tool aims to capture subjective experiences of stuttering and fluency, providing valuable insights into the cognitive and emotional aspects of speech production. We look forward to what we can learn from it toward developing a valid instrument that can measure an interoceptive awareness of stuttering. Please contact Dr. Andrew Bowers or Leslie for a link to the questionnaire. She recently had a poster presentation accepted at the ASHA convention for this year and plans to attend the conference in November.

Eddie Brown completes two published projects at the University of Tennessee Health Sciences Center and begins his dissertation work

Andrew Bowers April 15, 2025 3 Comments Research Highlights

Eddie Brown is a doctoral candidate and has made significant strides in his research at the University of Tennessee Health Sciences Center. He recently completed two published projects that contribute to our understanding of EEG correltes of stuttering and its applications in clinical settings. Eddie's work has already been recognized for its innovative approach and implications. He is now embarking on his dissertation research, which will further explore novel approaches to therapy for adults who stutter and track outcomes using new meassures. Eddie collected data in his second project that for the first time allows use to examine EEG oscillations in a naturalistic question and answer task that has yeilded a number of novel and interesting insights about how task demands are an important consideration in determining power differences in the alpha and beta bands in adults who stuttter. The data he collected is also yeilding new inisgights into multiple processes mediating repsonses to those tasks and measures of stuttering.
